01 Understand

Gather Requirements Problem Definition

A client approached us with the task of developing a new cloud storage application to compete with some big-name competitors in the space (Google Drive, Dropbox, Box, Evernote, Pinterest).

To meet business goals and objectives, the application needed to include the following features:

  • saving content you find on the web (links, images, videos, etc.)
  • organizing content using things like categories, tags, groups, and/or folders
  • creating content (notes, documents, maybe spreadsheets)
  • uploading files (videos, images, PDFs, etc.) from a computer or mobile device

The client also wanted to explore collaboration features as well:

  • sharing a single item/groups of items with someone else (and vice-versa)
  • connecting with other users for real-time collaboration in notes or documents

With these requirements in mind, we first needed to conduct user research to inform our strategy, and understand user needs were.

Competitive Analysis

To help clarify what direction the cloud storage application should go towards, I began with a competitive analysis to understand what solutions already existed in the market. I looked at three major players: Google Drive, Dropbox, and Evernote.

View Competitive Analysis

Dropbox: Offers a more robust feature set for sharing files (i.e. password protected links), and people like Dropbox to store and access files.

Google Drive: People like using Drive for content creation, and it integrates well with e-mail accounts and other applications.

Evernote: Positions itself as “the best note-taking app” and productivity tool -- unlike Google Drive and Dropbox, it focuses more on creating notes and managing your to-do’s.

User Surveys

I conducted a user survey to better understand users’ current likes and dislikes with cloud storage apps in the market.

View Survey

Key Insights

86% of users felt they currently spent too much time trying to sort through and organize their uploaded content.

55% of users responded they collaborate and work in the cloud “very often” (several times per week), indicating collaboration features would be important.

70%cited that the ability to quickly find and access the files they need was important.

User Interviews

I conducted follow-up interviews with three survey respondents as well. Below are some of the key findings:

Key Insights

Keeping things as simple and easy to use as possible is important if you want people to keep coming back and use the service.

The ability to set specific user permissions (aside from read, comment, and edit modes) can help differentiate our product.

User Personas

When analyzing the survey and interview data, two main types of users emerged: the Constant Collaborator (works off of multiple documents and collaborates/shares files often), and the Gatherer-Saver persona (someone who collects and uploads many files).

Dana

The Constant Collaborator

Age

32

Job

Startup project manager

Goals

  • Set user permissions to determine who can view, edit, or move files
  • Make sharing files more secure when sharing with people outside of the organization

Frustrations

  • User permission settings are not robust enough
  • Current user interface’s way of organizing and moving files does not feel as intuitive as on the desktop

Motivations

Dana constantly shares content with a range of people: clients, sub-contractors, and former colleagues (when she needs some advice) that requires settings differeng user permissions.

She wishes this process would give her more options than just “can view” or “can edit.” Sometimes, she only wants people to leave comments, but has to give them full editing permissions in order to do so.

Caleb

The Gatherer-Saver

Age

28

Job

PhD Research Assistant

Goals

  • Upload and organize research articles, scanned book pages, and other data
  • Create new word documents within the app to take notes

Frustrations

  • Cannot add tags or labels to content as a different way to organize content
  • Accessing content from mobile when on the go is not as robust as from a laptop or desktop

Motivations

As a PhD student, Caleb needs to organize massive amounts of research articles, scanned documents, and other data he compiles.

To carry around all of these documents wherever he goes is messy, so Caleb lives off of cloud storage. However, he wishes there were a way to add tags to his content--so if he searches “Post-War 1950,” he can see across all folders the content related to this topic.

Test + Redesign

Usability Testing

Using the low-fidelity prototype, I conducted two in-person and two remote usability tests. Up until this point (and based on initial user research), the product’s features had focused on supporting collaboration and categorizing the content.

Key Insights

Participants commented on how the side panel features lacked a “project management” component that would aid in their workflow. I uncovered how people’s motivations for accessing the cloud storage apps isn’t simply just to store, view, and organize files -- but rather, it is a hub where they do their work (often across multiple files) in order to accomplish a project.

Design Changes

Based on this feedback, I knew I had to do something in order to make the app more “productivity” and “project management” focused. Since several users mentioned that they have to keep track of a lot of deadlines, I thought to add a “flagged” feature where you can flag documents that are more time-sensitive that need work.

Before

Sidebar panel included only Files, Shared, Starred, and Trash.

After

Added Flagged, Pinned, and Labels as additional features to better support project management.

Branding + Style Guide

Brand Story

With the information architecture in place, our product needed a brand and identity to reinforce our position as a simple-to-use cloud storage service focused on project management and collaboration.

Through a process of ideation, mind-mapping, and research, the product name “ideaLAB” was chosen.

The word “lab” brings forth an image of a workspace workspace that’s clean, well-organized, methodical. It’s also a place where creativity and experimentation reside, and there’s also a collaborative component to it as well -- it’s where both individuals and teams  carry out really great work.

The name ideaLAB reflects a platform that’s a structured workspace for all of your ideas, and helps you manage your projects and facilitates collaboration with others.

Logo

After several sketches, iterations, and preference testing, the logo for ideaLAB was created.

We found that a wordmark logo was more recognizable and legible. The logo incorporates a flask and lightbulb as the letter “A.” The logo was also kept black and white to convey the simplicity of the brand, and how ideaLAB can be thought of as your blank canvas to get started on any project.

Color

The colors chosen for the brand were blue and yellow. The blue conveys a sense of calm (which helps if people are stressed while working), trust, and security, while the yellow injects a sense of action and creativity to keep from feeling too dull.

Typorgraphy

For the brand typeface, we use Poppins because of its friendly and approachable appearance -- we wanted ideaLAB to feel like an inviting space for ideas and projects. For the complementary typeface, we selected Helvetica Neue for its more “no-nonsense” straighter edges, to convey a sense of structure and precision.

05 Test

Usability Testing Design Changes

Key Insights

  1. Overall, no one had any issues with performing the tasks flows for the pin and flag feature. Yay!
  2. Continue to focus on simplying steps within user flows where possible.
  3. Provide ways to “undo” actions easily.

Design Changes

Change #1: Simplifying user flows

Problem: There were still too many steps involved for users to quickly create a new note. The user flow was was to click “New,” select “New note,” and then the user was forced to decide where they wanted to create the new note. After doing so, they had to click on the note itself again in order to access it and start writing. This was far too cumbersome.

Before

Cumbersome user flow that prevented users from quickly creating a new note.

After

Improved user flow that involves only two steps before you can get to jotting down your ideas. The user flow was changed so that once you click “New note” the new page automatically pops up, and you decide where to organize it later.

Change #2: Provide ways to undo actions

Problem: While users didn’t have a problem flagging or pinning an item, they commented on how they didn’t see a way to “un-flag” or “un-pin” an item if they no longer needed that feature.

Before

There was no way to remove a flagged item if the user clicked on or even hovered over the flag.

After

To allow users a way to un-flag an item, I implemented a hover effect so that anytime you hover over the flagged folder, ot the folder name, the three vertical dots will show up for you to click on. Once clicking on that, the very first option at the top is to remove.

Summary

Since the cloud storage market is already quite saturated with big players such as Google Drive, Dropbox, and Evernote, it was challenging to think through how a new cloud storage app could differentiate from the competition. ideaLAB taught me the importance of grounding your design in user research and validating ideas through user testing. There were many points throughout the project where I anticipated a certain reaction or preference from users, but actual research proved me wrong.

Knowing that users wanted something simple, easy, and efficient to support their projects provided the direction and clarity when determining what features would be useful, and how the task sequences should flow.

Moving forward, ideaLAB could be further refined by expanding out the mobile prototype, so that accessing files from mobile is as seamless as it is on the desktop.
